Fingerprints

  • Morgan – a circular fingerprint where the maximum size of the fingerprint set is pre-defined by a specified radius. Fingerprints are generated from all possible paths in a molecule up to the specified radius.


    further details on circular fingerprints please refer to Rogers, D.; Hahn, M. “Extended-Connectivity Fingerprints.” J. Chem. Inf. Model. 2010, 50, 742–754.


  • Atom-pair – a path-based fingerprint in which the set of fragments from a compound is generated from all possible pairs of non-hydrogen atoms separated by an interatomic distance. The fingerprint description also includes the nearest neighbors of the two atoms and specifies the presence of π electrons where applicable.


    Carhart, R.E.; Smith, D.H.; Venkataraghavan, R. “Atom Pairs as Molecular Features in Structure-Activity Studies: Definition and Applications.” J. Chem. Info. Comput. Sci. 1985, 25, 64-73.

    • Clustering algorithm

  • Butina Exclusion Sphere – the algorithm begins by a Tanimoto similarity (described below) input by the user. Each compound is then assigned a certain number of neighbors where each neighbor shares at least the inputted similarity level. All compounds are then ranked by decreasing number of neighbors with the compound having the most neighbors designated as the centroid of the first cluster. All compounds belonging to this first cluster are excluded from being apart of all other clusters. This process is repeated down the list until the remaining compounds have no neighbors and are thus designated as singletons


    Butina, D. “Unsupervised Data Base Clustering Based on Daylight’s Fingerprint and Tanimoto Similarity: A Fast and Automated Way To Cluster Small and Large Data Sets.” J. Chem. Inf. Comput. Sci. 1998. 39, 747 – 750.

    • Pan Assay Interference Compounds (PAINS)

  • Compounds possessing chemical functionality which have demonstrated to cause issues in lead discovery - such as interaction with biomolecules in undesirable ways, interference with assay readouts, or production of misleading screening results. Eli Lily developed a set of rules divided into 17 classes for the identification of troublesome compounds.


    The full report and description of the Eli Lily rules can be found in Bruns, R.F.; Watson, I.A. “Rules for Identifying Potentially Reactive or Promiscuous Compounds.” J. Med. Chem. 2012, 55, 9763 – 9722.
